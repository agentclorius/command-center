# From Sustainability to Strategy: Trade-In Is No Longer Just About Being Green

_Draft for Returna blog — February 2026_

---

For years, device trade-in programs lived in the sustainability department. A nice-to-have. A checkbox for the annual ESG report.

That era is over.

At CCS Insight's Circular Markets 2026 event, the message was unmistakable: trade-in has moved from corporate responsibility to competitive strategy.

## The Strategic Shift

Listen to how carriers talk about trade-in today:

- "Guaranteed value" offers that lock in customer loyalty
- "Future value" programs that shape purchasing decisions
- Upgrade incentives designed to increase lifetime value

This isn't sustainability language. This is go-to-market strategy.

The carriers leading in customer retention aren't just offering better networks — they're offering better end-of-life economics. Trade-in has become a competitive weapon.

## Why Now?

Three forces are converging:

**1. Device prices keep climbing**

Premium smartphones now cost €1,000+. Laptops even more. Consumers and enterprises alike are looking for ways to offset that cost. Trade-in value matters more than ever.

**2. Upgrade cycles are lengthening**

People hold onto devices longer. That means carriers and OEMs need new levers to drive upgrades. Trade-in offers create urgency without discounting.

**3. Sustainability pressure is real**

NIS2 directives, CSRD reporting, Right to Repair legislation — the regulatory environment is tightening. Companies that can demonstrate circular practices gain real advantage.

## Beyond Smartphones

The mobile industry is the laboratory. It's proven that trade-in programs work at scale, that consumers respond to value recovery, and that refurbished devices expand rather than cannibalize markets.

Now the model is extending:

- **Enterprise IT:** Laptops, monitors, peripherals from distributed workforces
- **Consumer electronics:** Tablets, wearables, gaming consoles
- **B2B equipment:** Network hardware, medical devices, industrial tools

Every category that learned from smartphones is seeing the same pattern: trade-in isn't a cost center, it's a value driver.

## The New Table Stakes

For enterprises, circular programs are becoming procurement requirements. RFPs now include questions about end-of-life handling, data destruction certification, and sustainability reporting.

Companies without clear answers are losing deals.

For carriers and retailers, trade-in quality is becoming a brand differentiator. Consumers are learning to ask: "What will this device be worth in two years? How easy will it be to return?"

The companies that answer those questions best will win market share.
